Facts

Main Causes

Confusing Labels

People throw away good food because they mix up "best before" and "use by" dates on packaging.

Oversized Portions

Large restaurant servings and big packages lead to uneaten food and plate waste.

Store Overstock

Supermarkets waste millions of tons of food each year by over-ordering products.

Lack of Accessible Food Rescue and Recycling Services

Sometimes we all forget about some vegetables we put at the back of the fridge. When we do remember, some vegetables are droopy and soft. So it went to the trash can. Instead of wasting, use these as compost or use them for recycling

Consequences of food waste

Health Problems

Food waste can cause serious poisoning if spoiled food is eaten.

Harms Nature

Rotting food makes methane gas that hurts our planet.

Money Wasted

People throw away hundreds of dollars worth of food.

Forgotten Food

Vegetables often rot unseen in fridge drawers.

Food Waste Issues

1/3 of all food

17% of global food production is wasted approximately.

More deatils information

About 1/3 of all food made in the world is never eaten. In 2022, over 1 billion meals were thrown away every day. That’s nearly 19% of all food available to people at that time. This isn’t just about food it also wastes water, money, and energy. On top of that, when food rots in landfills, it creates gases that harm our planet and make climate change worsen.

Household Waste

21% of food comes from homes and 10% from consumer-facing businesses.

More details information

Most food waste actually comes from our homes. Around 60% of all food waste in the world about 631 million tonnes was from households in 2022. That means each person, on average, wasted about 79 kilograms of food that year. People often forget leftovers, buy too much, or let food spoil before eating.

Food waste by human consumption

14% of food produced for human consumption never reaches consumers.

More deatails information

Around 14% of food made for people consumption but even makes it to them and these numbers show that a lot of food is wasted at different stages—from farms to stores to homes. This highlights the need to manage food better and use more sustainable ways to produce and consume it.

Econiomic Impact

Global food waste has an estimated economic cost of about $1 trillion annually.

More deatails information

Wasting food costs the world a lot of money—about $1 trillion every year. This huge amount includes the money lost at every step of the food process, from growing and transporting food to selling it in stores and throwing it away at home.

Edible food

Food waste makes up 24% of landfill waste and 22% of combusted solid waste.

More deatails information

In the United States, food waste is the biggest type of trash that ends up in landfills and is burned in incinerators. About 1 out of every 4 pounds of garbage sent to landfills is food. Also, nearly 1 out of every 5 pounds of trash burned in waste facilities is food. This means a lot of food is being thrown away instead of being used or composted.

Hunger Impact

40% of the entire US food supply is wasted, recording 325 pounds of food wasted per person approximately.

More deatails information

About 40% of all the food in the United States is wasted. That means each person throws away about 325 pounds of food every year. This shows that a lot of food is being lost or thrown out across the country. It's a reminder that we all need to do our part to waste less food and make better choices to help the environment.

Food Waste Cycle

1. Production

Food is grown on farms. Waste occurs due to overproduction, pests, or cosmetic standards.

2. Distribution

Food is transported to stores. Waste happens from poor storage, expired stock, or damaged goods.

3. Consumption

People buy and eat food. Waste comes from overbuying, spoilage, or plate leftovers.

4. Disposal

Food is thrown away. In landfills, it rots and releases methane (a harmful greenhouse gas).

5. Decomposition

Composting turns waste into soil nutrients. Landfill waste harms the environment.
